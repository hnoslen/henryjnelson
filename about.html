About Me

Hello, my name is Henry J. Nelson and I'm an undergraduate physics and math major at a small liberal arts college. I really enjoy working on almost anything in the above catagories. There is always something that I'm working on so I thought I'd make a place where I could share all the ongoing and completed projects to better improve my book-keeping and maybe help someone else out in the process.  Hopefully you will find something that's interesting and educational. This site is my current project and hopefully it will improve as time goes on and I learn what I'm doing better, so hopfully the building of this site will be somewhere on this site if you want to try something similar.

Below I will talk a little about my background in each area of the site so you can maybe get up to speed if you don't understand something by using the resources I did. I will try and make everything accessable to a beginner  in whatever we're doing as that's usually where I am, but at some point everyone gets lost so don't be afraid to learn on your own from other sources.

Physics

My physics background is easily reproducible. When I was young, I loved all things science-fiction. At some point I recieved the book "The Physics of Star Trek" by Lawrence M. Krauss and it blew my mind. Many things in the magical stories of Star Trek we actually possible! This book also gave me my first glance at the more interesting facets of physics like reletivity and quantum mechanics getting me hooked on the magical nature of the world. From there I consumed everything about physics that was produced for a popular audience. I loved watching Nova and Scientific American Frontiers as well as reading popular science books like "Six Easy Pieces" which are excerpts from the Feynman Lectures on Physics and "Alice in Quantumland" which are both very understandable for a novice and I highly recommend them. After that I finally got to a point in school where I could take physics classes and I took a general physics course and a very short introductory class into particle physics. After that I started college and started moving into higher levels, but hopefully most of the concepts should still be graspable (maybe not the math, but the general intuition at least).

Math Background

I always sucked at math. My school always did the seperate classes for students based on test scores (MAPs or MCAs or something), so needless to say I was always in the lowest teir math class. I don't know how it happened, but between grades 5 and 6, I moved from the lowest level math class to the highest and ended up struggling quite a bit to keep up. But I persevered and made it through the rest of my schooling unimpeded by math. After getting to college math became the perfect tool for my other passions like physics and the theoretical computer science so I continued taking classes. When I finally got to the higher levels of mathematics (Cobinatorics,Graph Theory, and Abstract Algebra) math started to be incredibly cool not just as a tool, but also for itself. At this point I now love math and the wonderfully logical arguments used in proofs, but I still suck at it. I just suck at a higher lever of math now.

Computer Science Background

My computer science knowledge has definitly had the fastest growth of any of my abilities. Just a few years ago I knew nothing about tech except maybe how to hook up a tv. About the time I was a teenager, my friends and classmates started having an iPod Touch or something similar, so I had to have one too.  But for some reason I had an aversion to Apple and ended up buying myself a Nokia N900 (which had many more features than the iPod Touch). The N900 had Maemo as its operating system which was a linux variant. Pretty soon I was playing around with the terminal and built-in programming suite and writing simple programs. I originally just typed in what I wanted to happen and found that in many instances it would just work (later I found this was the user friendlyness of Python). Once I learned how versitile Python was I took the Udacity mooc, Introduction to Computer Science, which gave me a good basis. From there I started working on more complicated programs eventually moving to programming with the Raspberry Pi. As I tried to do more applied programming projects it was necessary to learn other languages like C/C++, Java, and Matlab. All of these I learned on my own using the vast resources online. Specifically C/C++ and Java can be learned from almost any of the online sources. For Matlab I recommend reading through the wonderful documentation that comes with it.
